## Abstract

Excitation–inhibition (E/I) imbalance has been proposed as the translational hub linking molecular Alzheimer's disease (AD) pathology to large-scale network dysfunction and cognitive decline (Maestú, 2021). Because E/I cannot be measured non-invasively in humans, brain network models (BNMs) that embed neural mass models (NMMs) on diffusion-derived structural connectomes are increasingly used to infer it from EEG, MEG, and fMRI (Lu, 2022). This review synthesizes the mathematical foundations, inference methods, and empirical evidence underpinning this program, distinguishing normal aging from AD throughout. We trace the move from the heuristic sigmoid NMM1 to the exact mean-field NMM2 derived from QIF neurons (Clusella, 2022), the adiabatic potential-landscape formulation that yields closed-form state dwell times (Cooray, 2023), and the critical validity boundary (lambda roughly 0.70–0.85) outside which mean-field predictions diverge from spiking ground truth (Deschle, 2021). Empirically, forward models map amyloid-PET to inhibitory time-constant lengthening (tau_i 14→50 ms) producing hub-dependent alpha-to-theta slowing on N=33 ADNI-3 data (Stefanovski, 2019); inverse models fit per-subject couplings to FC predicting cognition in N=124 (Zimmermann, 2018); Stuart-Landau models identify theta-band AEC as the most sensitive early-AD hyperexcitability marker (SCD r=0.697, MCI r=0.587; Stam, 2023); and data assimilation tracks sub-second E/I in healthy sleep (Yokoyama, 2023). We argue this convergence reflects shared modeling priors rather than independent corroboration: E/I is never directly measured, mean-field validity is violated in the disease-relevant regimes, no virtual electrophysiology is validated against same-subject recordings, and no normal-aging E/I baseline exists. The framework is best positioned as a theoretical roadmap requiring validation, not established biomarker science.

**Keywords:** excitation–inhibition balance; neural mass model; The Virtual Brain; Alzheimer's disease; normal aging; EEG/MEG; data assimilation; functional connectivity

## 1. Introduction

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is increasingly conceptualized not merely as a story of progressive neuronal loss but as a disorder of network dynamics in which an imbalance between neuronal excitation and inhibition (E/I) occupies a pivotal, causal position. In the translational framework articulated by Maestú (2021), E/I imbalance functions as the central hub that links molecular pathology to macroscale network failure: amyloid-beta (Abeta) oligomers impair glutamate reuptake and selectively damage inhibitory interneurons, removing the braking action that fast-spiking GABAergic cells normally exert on pyramidal populations, thereby producing local hyperexcitability. This synaptic-level derangement is hypothesized to propagate upward into band-specific, particularly theta-band, hypersynchronization that predicts later network collapse and cognitive decline (Maestú, 2021). The appeal of the E/I construct is that it offers a single, mechanistically interpretable axis along which molecular, electrophysiological, and cognitive observations can in principle be aligned, and along which candidate therapeutics that modulate excitability (for example NMDA antagonists or anti-epileptic agents) can be rationally targeted.

The central methodological problem is that E/I balance cannot be measured directly and non-invasively in the living human brain. Intracellular recording, ex-vivo electrophysiology, and histological quantification of interneuron loss are inaccessible in vivo, and even proxies such as magnetic resonance spectroscopy of GABA and glutamate, or paired-pulse transcranial magnetic stimulation, are spatially coarse and rarely acquired in the cohorts of interest. This is why the field has turned to model-based inference: brain network models (BNMs) that place neural mass models (NMMs) or phenomenological oscillators as nodes on a diffusion-MRI-derived structural connectome, simulate observable signals such as the EEG, MEG, or fMRI BOLD response, and then invert the model to recover hidden physiological parameters, including local E/I (Lu, 2022). Descriptive graph-theoretic analyses can reveal what changes in a diseased network, but they cannot speak to why; generative BNMs, by contrast, embed an explicit mechanism and can be run forward (from assumed pathology to predicted signal) or inverted (from observed signal to inferred parameters), while dynamic causal modelling offers causal inference but does not scale to whole-brain resolution (Lu, 2022).

Throughout this review we maintain a distinction that the primary literature treats only obliquely: normal aging versus AD. The available syntheses frame normal aging as dominated by diffuse loss of structural-connectome weight, where the global coupling parameter G carries most of the dynamic burden, whereas AD is hypothesized to add local synaptic derailment in inhibitory-to-excitatory (I-E) and excitatory-to-inhibitory (E-I) couplings together with spatially heterogeneous Abeta and tau deposition, with amnestic mild cognitive impairment (aMCI) occupying a transitional zone of highest parameter variance interpreted as a competition between compensation and collapse (Zimmermann, 2018; Stefanovski, 2019). We stress at the outset that this aging-versus-AD framing is an interpretive hypothesis assembled across studies rather than a result demonstrated by any single primary paper, and that a normal-aging E/I baseline established in amyloid-negative, age-matched controls is essentially absent from the corpus. This review therefore proceeds by first establishing the mathematical foundations of the models (Section 2), then the inference machinery (Section 3), then the empirical evidence across aging and AD (Section 4) and its molecular-to-network and translational interpretation (Section 5), before turning to a detailed critical accounting of limitations and validity boundaries (Section 6), future directions (Section 7), and conclusions (Section 8).

## 2. Mathematical Foundations of Neural Mass Models

The credibility of any E/I inference rests on the fidelity of the underlying node model, and here the foundational distinction is between heuristic and exact neural mass models. The heuristic NMM1, exemplified by the classical Jansen-Rit and Wilson-Cowan lineage, assumes that the population mean firing rate r is a static nonlinear sigmoid of the mean membrane potential v. Clusella (2022) demonstrates that this assumption is not a free modelling choice but a limiting case: NMM1 is the infinitely-slow-synapse limit of an exact mean-field theory. The exact NMM2 is derived from a population of quadratic integrate-and-fire (QIF) neurons via a Lorentzian ansatz in the N→infinity limit (the Montbrio-Pazo-Roxin reduction), which closes the ensemble into two coupled ordinary differential equations in which the firing rate is no longer slaved to the potential but is itself a dynamical variable: tau_m r' = Delta/(pi tau_m) + 2rv and tau_m v' = eta − (pi r tau_m)^2 + v^2 + tau_m J s + I_E(t), where s is a second-order synaptic variable (Clusella, 2022). The qualitative consequence for AD modelling is consequential: through the ratio of membrane to synaptic time constants (tau_m/tau_s), NMM2 produces self-sustained interneuron-gamma (ING) oscillations in purely inhibitory networks, a regime that the static-sigmoid structure of NMM1 cannot reproduce. Because gamma rhythmicity and parvalbumin-positive interneuron pathology are central to AD circuit dysfunction, this argues for preferring exact NMM2 node dynamics over standard Jansen-Rit when constructing AD virtual brains (Clusella, 2022). We note, however, that the explicit closed-form ODE for the second-order synaptic variable s and its precise dependence on tau_s are described only qualitatively in the available material, so this should be carried as a partially unverified specification.

A second foundational advance addresses how fast oscillatory dynamics give rise to the slow state transitions that characterize resting-state and pathological brain activity. Cooray (2023) applies an adiabatic timescale separation, distinguishing fast EEG phase dynamics from slow amplitude modulation, and through a second-order perturbation expansion recasts the slow mean-rate dynamics as a gradient flow on a scalar potential U with additive noise: dI = −grad U dt + sigma dB. The corresponding Fokker-Planck steady-state density takes the closed form p_st = (1/Z) exp(−2U/sigma^2), which in turn yields closed-form mean exit and dwell times between brain states such as resting versus seizure-like regimes. Critically, the anatomical synaptic coupling matrix G directly parameterizes U, so that AD-related synapse loss reshapes the landscape and predicts abnormal state-occupancy statistics without recourse to full stochastic simulation (Cooray, 2023). This framework establishes a definite micro(fast)→macro(slow) causal direction, in contrast to phenomenological models such as the Epileptor that inject a slow permittivity variable by hand without micro-synaptic justification (Cooray, 2023). We emphasize that this is theoretical work: the explicit functional form of U(G) and the perturbation coefficients are not fully given, no specific dwell-time numbers are reported, and the framework has never been applied to AD patient data, leaving dwell-time biomarkers an unused capability.

The third and most consequential foundation is the validity boundary of mean-field reduction itself. Deschle (2021) defines an E/I topology parameter lambda = Nexc/(Ninh+Nexc) and compares the true mean of a leaky integrate-and-fire (LIF) network against perturbatively derived NMMs. The crude closure assumption that a single neuron tracks the population mean fails badly under both high-frequency synchronization and full desynchronization; NMMs faithfully represent the underlying network only near the low-frequency synchronization onset, roughly lambda in 0.70–0.85, with spectral predictions diverging (significant chi-squared) outside this critical transition band (Deschle, 2021). We preserve the 'roughly' qualifier deliberately, since the precise numerical boundaries, the chi-squared magnitudes, and the LIF network sizes are not specified in the available material. This validity constraint is the single most important epistemic safeguard in the entire enterprise, because the AD regions of greatest clinical interest, namely strongly hyper-synchronized and strongly de-synchronized cortex, are precisely those most likely to lie outside the band where the models can be trusted. We further flag that the formal mapping from Deschle's lambda to the E/I metrics used elsewhere (Yokoyama's mE/I = A/(A+B); Stam's bifurcation parameter a) is inferential and not established in any source, and that the homogeneity assumption embedded in all mean-field derivations is violated by AD's spatially heterogeneous plaque and tangle load without any AD-specific quantification of the resulting bias against spiking ground truth.

## 3. Multiscale Virtual-Brain Modeling and E/I Inference Methods

Brain network models share a common anatomy: dMRI/DWI-derived structural connectomes provide the edges, while NMMs or simplified oscillators provide the nodes, and the assembled system simulates fMRI BOLD or M/EEG and permits inverse inference of hidden physiological parameters such as local E/I (Lu, 2022). Within this scaffold, descriptive graph-theoretic BNMs (hubs, path length) reveal what changes but offer no causal mechanism, whereas generative BNMs encode an explicit dynamical hypothesis; dynamic causal modelling provides causal effective-connectivity inference but does not scale to whole-brain resolution, leaving a gap between mechanism and scale that whole-brain BNMs attempt to fill at the cost of directed-causal interpretability (Lu, 2022).

The choice of node dynamics is best understood as task-conditional rather than as a contest with a single winner. For macroscale functional-connectivity dysfunction, the Stuart-Landau oscillator is preferred: it is computationally cheap and tunes E/I through a single bifurcation parameter a that can be optimized to maximize amplitude-envelope-correlation fidelity (Lu, 2022; Stam, 2023). For questions targeting a specific synaptic or pharmacological mechanism, biophysical NMMs such as Jansen-Rit or the reduced Wong-Wang model are preferred because they carry explicit excitatory and inhibitory postsynaptic potentials (tau_e, tau_i, synaptic gains) suited to data assimilation (Lu, 2022; Stefanovski, 2019). We present this as a decision rule with two explicit caveats: the Stuart-Landau parameter a is a phenomenological scalar that conflates all sources of E/I change and cannot be mapped to specific synaptic parameters without an additional translation step, and no head-to-head validation has confirmed that the Stuart-Landau and Jansen-Rit/Wong-Wang parameterizations capture the same underlying physical E/I shift (Stam, 2023; Stefanovski, 2019). Convergence among models that share the prior that an E/I axis drives FC is therefore convergent hypothesis-testing, not independent corroboration.

E/I can be recovered either by inverse fitting to a static target or by time-resolved data assimilation. The inverse approach is exemplified by Zimmermann (2018), who performs a parameter-space search over global coupling G and local E-E, E-I, and I-E couplings to best fit each subject's empirical FC, then relates the fitted parameters to cognition. The data-assimilation approach is exemplified by Yokoyama (2023), whose variational-Bayes constrained ensemble Kalman filter (vbcEnKF) assimilates scalp EEG sample-by-sample into a nonlinear NMM, with variational-Bayes noise adaptation preventing filter divergence under nonstationary observation noise, and computes a model E/I index mE/I = A/(A+B) from independently estimated excitatory gain A and inhibitory gain B at sub-second resolution; crucially it remains stable below 20 Hz, where the gamma-dependent 1/f E/I-slope method fails (Yokoyama, 2023). A distinct hybrid strategy is offered by Schirner (2018), who injects empirical EEG source activity as excitatory postsynaptic current (EPSC) directly into connectome nodes and predicts 20-minute subject-specific fMRI; the mechanistic core is inhibitory half-wave rectification, in which the biological constraint that firing rates cannot go negative (rate ≥ 0) converts high-amplitude alpha envelopes into low-frequency rate fluctuations that drive slow resting-state networks via neurovascular coupling (Schirner, 2018). This nonlinear physiological bound is essential to reproducing macro dynamics, but the requirement for simultaneous empirical EEG injection reduces model autonomy and precludes purely prospective or drug-simulation use. Finally, the forward direction is exemplified by Stefanovski (2019, 2021), in which assumed molecular pathology is imposed on the model to predict a signal, completing a forward-and-inverse toolbox that spans molecular input through network simulation to output fitting and in-silico pharmacology.

## 4. Empirical Evidence Across Normal Aging and Alzheimer's Disease

The empirical corpus comprises four studies that, taken together, span MEG, EEG, and fMRI but differ markedly in cohort, modality, and disease stage. Stam (2023) coupled a 78-node Stuart-Landau oscillator model to a structural connectome and swept the bifurcation parameter a (the E/I proxy) and global coupling G, comparing simulated FC to real MEG from 18 subjects with subjective cognitive decline (SCD) and 18 with MCI. The corrected amplitude-envelope correlation (AECc) in the theta band (4–8 Hz) best matched empirical networks at high E/I (the hyperexcitable regime, a > 0, where a noisy fixed point transitions to a limit cycle) combined with moderate coupling (G ~ 1), yielding SCD r = 0.697 and MCI r = 0.587 (Stam, 2023). The central inference is that theta-band AEC is the most sensitive macroscale marker of early-AD hyperexcitability and that AEC and phase-consistency carry different E/I sensitivities, so FC measures act as surrogates for hyperexcitability but are not interchangeable (Stam, 2023). The strength of this evidence is bounded: the cohorts are small (18 each, 36 total), contain no diagnosed AD dementia, the higher SCD correlation may reflect cohort scatter, and hyperexcitability is inferred via best-fit model parameters rather than directly measured, with no p-values or confidence intervals reported.

Yokoyama (2023) provides ground-truth-adjacent validation but in normal physiology, not disease. Applying the vbcEnKF to whole-night sleep EEG from 19 healthy adults, it recovered mE/I = A/(A+B) at sub-second resolution and tracked physiologically expected shifts (mE/I rising in deep NREM, an excitation-dominant state, and falling in REM, an inhibition-dominant state), transitions that the traditional 1/f E/I-slope detected not at all (Yokoyama, 2023). This is a method-validation study: the cohort is healthy, the inference is single-channel, it has not been extended to whole-brain high-dimensional assimilation, and its relevance to AD is by extension only.

The two studies with diagnosed AD adopt opposite modelling directions. Stefanovski (2019) built personalized TVB Jansen-Rit models for ADNI-3 (N = 33; 10 AD, 8 MCI, 15 HC), holding the whole-brain structural connectome fixed at the healthy mean to isolate the molecular effect, and mapping per-region AV-45 Abeta-PET SUVR through a sigmoid that lengthened the local inhibitory time constant tau_i from a default 14 ms up to 50 ms above a ~1.4 SUVR threshold (integrated by a Heun scheme at a 5 ms step). The virtual EEG/LFP showed marked alpha-to-theta slowing (peak toward ~4 Hz) plus chaotic dynamics, and two controls supported a hub-propagation mechanism: shuffling the spatial distribution of Abeta kept the slowing concentrated at network hubs, while a spatially homogeneous Abeta load produced no slowing at all, implicating heterogeneity plus propagation through structural hubs such as the DMN/precuneus (Stefanovski, 2019). The underlying dynamical mechanism is bistability, with coexisting alpha and theta limit cycles into whose bifurcation zone the amyloid load drives the system. By contrast, Zimmermann (2018) implemented the inverse direction in the MAS cohort (N = 124; 73 HC, 35 aMCI, 16 AD)—the largest of the four—using per-subject SC and a parameter-space search over G and local E-E, E-I, and I-E couplings to fit each subject's empirical FC, then mapping fitted parameters to 6 cognitive scores by partial least squares. In the limbic subnetwork, higher G and higher excitatory couplings (E-E, E-I) correlated negatively with cognition while local inhibition (I-E) correlated positively, and fitted model parameters predicted cognition better than raw SC or FC; the difference between limbic-subnet-optimal and whole-brain-optimal parameters defined an 'embeddedness' index proposed as a biomarker of network-integration breakdown (Zimmermann, 2018).

Regarding the aging-versus-AD contrast, only Stefanovski (2019) and Zimmermann (2018) include diagnosed AD; Stam (2023) stops at the pre-dementia SCD/MCI range and Yokoyama (2023) is healthy-only. No primary paper empirically compares a healthy-aging E/I trajectory against AD with biomarker-confirmed, amyloid-negative age-matched controls. The framing that aging reflects diffuse SC weight loss (global G dominant) while AD adds local I-E/E-I derailment plus Abeta/tau heterogeneity, with aMCI showing the highest variance in a compensation-versus-collapse transition, is therefore a medium-confidence interpretive synthesis rather than a measured result (Zimmermann, 2018; Stefanovski, 2019).

## 5. Molecular-to-Network Mechanisms and Translational Implications

The translational spine of this literature is a multiscale chain running from molecular pathology to cognition: Abeta oligomers impair glutamate reuptake and damage inhibitory interneurons, producing pyramidal-cell hyperexcitability and E/I imbalance, which manifests as band-specific (especially theta) hypersynchronization, network failure, and ultimately cognitive decline, with E/I imbalance as the unifying hub (Maestú, 2021). The operationalization of the first link is concrete: Abeta selectively harms fast-spiking inhibitory interneurons, reducing GABA_A postsynaptic current and removing pyramidal-cell braking, an event modelled in Jansen-Rit by lengthening the inhibitory time constant tau_i from 14 ms up to 50 ms in regions where AV-45 SUVR exceeds the ~1.4 threshold (Stefanovski, 2019). A second mechanistic insight is that the resulting EEG slowing is not a passive readout of local neuronal death: spatial heterogeneity of Abeta plus propagation through structural hubs is necessary, since homogeneous Abeta yields no slowing and a few high-Abeta hub regions broadcast abnormal slow oscillations through the connectome (Stefanovski, 2019). This distinguishes AD from uniform neurodegeneration and links naturally to the spatially dissociated synchrony pattern of early AD—anterior hypersynchrony co-existing with posterior hyposynchrony—reported as a signature of E/I imbalance (Maestú, 2021), though the mechanistic origin of posterior hyposynchrony within the BNM framework is named rather than demonstrated.

The inverse evidence reinforces the same directionality: in Zimmermann (2018), higher excitatory couplings and global coupling in the limbic subnetwork track worse cognition while local inhibition tracks better cognition, and model-inferred parameters outpredict raw connectivity, supporting the claim that BNM-derived E/I captures clinically meaningful biology rather than fitting artifact. At the macroscale, theta-band AECc emerges as the most sensitive biomarker of early hyperexcitability, peaking at the dynamic critical point (Stam, 2023). A crucial and frequently neglected caveat applies to the tau_i operationalization: lengthening tau_i widens the inhibitory PSC and increases the area under its curve, which raises total integrated inhibitory charge—the opposite of the disinhibition narrative—so that a 'hyperexcitability' reading requires a relative local-excitability index that is never explicitly defined. Conflating 'slower inhibition' with 'less inhibition' is at minimum ambiguous and arguably mislabeled, and any mechanistic interpretation of tau_i lengthening must carry this artifact explicitly.

On the translational front, Stefanovski (2021) recommends a dual-hit forward-modelling strategy in which Abeta-PET lengthens tau_i (modelling hyperexcitability) while Tau-PET or atrophy maps lower local coupling (modelling neuronal death and disconnection), because single-molecule attribution to Abeta alone likely overestimates Abeta's pure dynamic effect given that tau correlates better with atrophy and cognition. We present dual-hit modelling as the recommended forward stance while flagging explicitly that it is not yet empirically validated: Stefanovski (2019), the main empirical paper, tested Abeta only with SC fixed at the healthy mean, and Tau-driven coupling reduction has never been tested against data. In-silico pharmacology completes the pipeline: virtual Memantine, modelled as a 25% reduction in the excitatory coupling parameter c31 to approximate NMDA-receptor antagonism, renormalized mean spectral frequency toward HC/MCI levels with the effect concentrating on hyperactive hubs (Stefanovski, 2019), and Levetiracetam is proposed as a candidate anti-hyperexcitability agent whose trials could be stratified using E/I-sensitive readouts such as theta AECc (Maestú, 2021; Stam, 2023). These remain proof-of-concept: there is no dose-response, no pharmacokinetics, no comparison to actual drug-trial outcomes, and the Levetiracetam-to-theta-AECc linkage is a medium-confidence synthesis across citations rather than a single established result. Across the entire chain, no single-subject study jointly validates histology, modelled tau_i, and EEG, so the molecular-to-network mechanism is convergent across frameworks and mechanistically plausible yet not end-to-end validated.

## 6. Limitations and Validity Boundaries

Five weaknesses are jointly fatal to any strong empirical reading. First, mean-field validity is violated in exactly the disease-relevant regions: Deschle (2021) confines NMM faithfulness to a narrow band (lambda roughly 0.70–0.85) near low-frequency synchronization onset, yet AD's defining signatures—anterior theta hypersynchrony and posterior alpha hyposynchrony—sit outside this band by construction (high-frequency synchronization and full desynchronization), where chi-squared divergence from LIF ground truth is significant, and no AD inversion study (Stefanovski, 2019; Zimmermann, 2018; Yokoyama, 2023; Stam, 2023) verifies that pathological nodes remain in-band, so the inference machinery is most unreliable precisely where the disease signal is strongest. Second, no study validates simulated electrophysiology against the same subjects' real recordings: Stefanovski (2019) had no concurrent EEG for ADNI-3 (N=33), so the alpha-to-theta slowing, hub propagation, and virtual-Memantine normalization are model-internal predictions, and a model parameterized to slow that is then observed to slow is a consistency demonstration, not validation. Third, hyperexcitability and E/I are never directly measured but always inferred as best-fit parameters (Stam's a; Zimmermann's couplings) or imposed as molecular mappings (Stefanovski's tau_i), with no intracellular, MRS, TMS, or histological ground truth in any AD cohort, creating a circular structure in which the model assumes an E/I axis, fits it to FC, and reports that E/I explains FC. Fourth, Zimmermann (2018) fits four coupled parameters (G, E-E, E-I, I-E) to a single static FC target with no conduction delays and no identifiability analysis; excitatory recurrence, feedforward excitation onto inhibition, and G can trade off to produce near-identical FC (sloppy-model degeneracy), so the reported parameter-cognition sign relationships may reflect where in a degenerate manifold the optimizer landed rather than identified physiology, and the PLS-to-cognition mapping inherits this non-uniqueness. Fifth, normal aging is never modelled as an independent variable: the aging-versus-AD framing exists only in editorial synthesis, no study recruited amyloid-negative subjects to characterize the healthy-aging E/I trajectory, HC groups function as statistical baselines rather than aging models (an unknown fraction may be preclinical AD), and without a documented aging baseline every claimed AD-specific deviation is confounded by unknown normal-aging variance.

Numerous major weaknesses compound these. The tau_i absolute-versus-relative inhibition artifact is methodologically ambiguous: lengthening tau_i from 14 to 50 ms raises the area under the inhibitory PSC and thus total integrated inhibition, the opposite of the disinhibition narrative, and the hyperexcitability claim requires a relative local-excitability index that is never defined (Stefanovski, 2019). Stefanovski (2019) holds the connectome fixed at the healthy mean for all groups, removing the well-established Tau-driven structural disconnection of AD and thereby overestimating Abeta's pure dynamic contribution; the dual-hit remedy (Stefanovski, 2021) concedes this but is itself untested. Theta-AECc as a stratification biomarker is unsupported: Stam (2023)'s correlations (SCD r=0.697, MCI r=0.587) come from a group-level model-vs-empirical match in cohorts of 18, with no diagnosed AD, no held-out test set, no replication, no confidence intervals or p-values, and no individual-level sensitivity/specificity/AUC, and r=0.587 leaves roughly 65% of MCI variance unexplained. Cohort sizes are insufficient for the AD-specific inferences, with no power analyses or multiple-comparison correction: diagnosed-AD n is 10 (Stefanovski, 2019), 16 (Zimmermann, 2018), and 0 in the other two, and a single r=0.697 with n=18 carries a 95% CI spanning roughly 0.34–0.87. The AD-specific BOLD/neurovascular confound is uncontrolled in Zimmermann (2018): amyloid angiopathy and altered neurovascular coupling change the hemodynamic transfer function in AD independently of neural activity, so NMM parameters fit to BOLD-FC may partly encode cerebrovascular burden rather than synaptic E/I, with the magnitude never estimated. Cross-framework convergence is overstated as independent corroboration: Stuart-Landau a, Jansen-Rit tau_i, and Wong-Wang couplings are different parameterizations of the same prior that an E/I axis drives FC, no head-to-head validation confirms they capture the same physical shift, and no formal mapping among Deschle's lambda, Yokoyama's mE/I=A/(A+B), and Stam's a is derived in any source—so this is circular triangulation, not replication. Yokoyama (2023) is single-channel, 19-subject, healthy-sleep method validation never tested in AD or extended to whole-brain assimilation, so treating it as evidence the EEG-based pipeline 'works' for AD is unjustified extrapolation. The SCD-to-aMCI-to-AD continuum is empirically discontinuous: no single study spans HC through AD with a consistent E/I measurement, and the aMCI 'highest-variance/compensation-vs-collapse' interpretation is editorial, not a reported statistic. Virtual Memantine is in-silico proof-of-concept presented with unwarranted clinical implications—a 25% c31 reduction normalizing an unvalidated spectrum with no dose-response, no pharmacokinetics, and no trial comparator (Stefanovski, 2019). Validation is essentially absent across the empirical chain, and no longitudinal or interventional data exist anywhere in the corpus, so every cognition link (Zimmermann, 2018) is cross-sectional and correlational and the causal arrow is asserted, never tested.

Several single-source findings merit recording. Numerical stability is unverified: Stefanovski (2019) uses a 5 ms Heun step while lengthening tau_i to 50 ms near a bifurcation, with no check for integration artifacts at the alpha-to-theta transition the result depends on. The homogeneity assumption underlying every mean-field NMM is violated by AD's spatially heterogeneous plaque/tangle load, and the Lorentzian ansatz presumes a quenched-disorder distribution that focal pathology disrupts, yet no AD-specific bias bound is derived. Finally, code and data availability are insufficient for independent reproduction (incompletely specified ADNI-3 pipeline parameters in Stefanovski, 2019; non-public MAS data in Zimmermann, 2018; undeposited vbcEnKF code in Yokoyama, 2023; unspecified MEG pipeline in Stam, 2023), and no cross-cohort or cross-scanner replication exists for any primary finding, since all four studies use different cohorts, scanners, atlases, and model classes.

## 7. Future Directions

The most urgent priority is to bound every E/I inference to the documented mean-field validity regime. Future studies should report, per node and per condition, whether the operating point lies within Deschle's critical band (lambda roughly 0.70–0.85), and should treat inferences drawn from hyper- or hypo-synchronized AD regions as provisional pending explicit in-band verification (Deschle, 2021). A complementary theoretical agenda is to derive AD-specific bias bounds for mean-field reduction under spatially heterogeneous, quenched-disorder pathology, since the homogeneity assumption is exactly the one AD violates.

Second, the field needs same-subject validation of virtual electrophysiology. The decisive experiment would acquire concurrent real EEG/MEG, PET, and structural MRI in a single AD cohort, fit the model, and test the simulated spectra and FC against the subjects' own recordings rather than against the assumptions used to build the model (Stefanovski, 2019). Within such a cohort, cross-method E/I estimates—Yokoyama's vbcEnKF mE/I, Zimmermann's inverse couplings, and Stam's bifurcation a—should be cross-validated against one another and, ideally, against an independent physiological anchor such as MRS-GABA/glutamate or TMS paired-pulse, to convert circular triangulation into genuine corroboration (Yokoyama, 2023; Zimmermann, 2018; Stam, 2023).

Third, the normal-aging baseline must be established before any aging-versus-AD claim can be sustained. This requires recruiting amyloid-negative, age-matched controls and characterizing the healthy-aging E/I and spectral trajectory directly, then layering biomarker-confirmed AD on top, and spanning the HC–SCD–aMCI–AD continuum within a single consistent modality and model so that the aMCI compensation-versus-collapse hypothesis can be tested as a measured variance statistic rather than asserted (Zimmermann, 2018; Stefanovski, 2019). Longitudinal and interventional designs are essential to convert correlational cognition links into causal ones.

Fourth, modelling practice should adopt several source-grounded recommendations while resolving open methodological questions. Exact NMM2 node dynamics (Clusella, 2022) are preferable to standard Jansen-Rit for capturing ING oscillations relevant to AD interneuron pathology, and theta-band AECc is a reasonable primary parameter-fitting cost function for macroscale screening (Stam, 2023). A formal head-to-head comparison of Stuart-Landau and Jansen-Rit/Wong-Wang parameterizations is needed to determine whether they capture the same physical E/I shift, alongside identifiability analyses (posterior covariance, profile likelihood, practical-identifiability checks) for multi-parameter inversions and the inclusion of conduction delays (Zimmermann, 2018). The dual-hit Abeta+Tau framework (Stefanovski, 2021) should be tested empirically, with a relative local-excitability index introduced to resolve the tau_i absolute-versus-relative inhibition ambiguity, and the AD-specific neurovascular confound should be explicitly modelled in all BOLD-based inferences. Finally, Cooray (2023)'s closed-form dwell-time and exit-time biomarkers should be computed on real AD electrophysiology to test whether state-occupancy statistics distinguish disease stages, and data assimilation should be extended from single-channel healthy sleep to multi-channel, whole-brain, waking AD recordings, while in-silico pharmacology should move beyond single-drug proof-of-concept toward dose-response and combination-therapy simulations validated against drug-response datasets (Yokoyama, 2023; Stefanovski, 2019; Maestú, 2021).

## 8. Conclusion

The model-based program to infer excitation–inhibition balance from EEG, MEG, and fMRI offers a mechanistically coherent and computationally tractable account of how molecular Alzheimer's pathology might produce large-scale network dysfunction and cognitive decline, with E/I imbalance as the translational hub (Maestú, 2021). Its mathematical foundations are genuinely advancing: the exact mean-field reduction supplies first-principles node dynamics capable of interneuron-gamma rhythms beyond the reach of heuristic models (Clusella, 2022); the adiabatic potential-landscape formulation yields closed-form state-transition statistics with a defensible micro-to-macro causal direction (Cooray, 2023); and the validity-boundary analysis provides an indispensable epistemic safeguard (Deschle, 2021). On these foundations a forward-and-inverse toolbox has been assembled, from amyloid-driven tau_i lengthening producing hub-dependent slowing (Stefanovski, 2019), through inverse coupling inference that outpredicts raw connectivity for cognition (Zimmermann, 2018), to theta-AECc as a candidate hyperexcitability marker (Stam, 2023) and sub-second data assimilation of E/I (Yokoyama, 2023).

Yet the convergence across these frameworks reflects shared modelling priors more than independent empirical corroboration. E/I is never measured, only fitted; mean-field inference is applied in exactly the pathological regimes where it is documented to be unreliable; no virtual electrophysiology is validated against same-subject recordings; the central inverse problem is degenerate without identifiability analysis; and the aging-versus-AD distinction that anchors the clinical framing rests on an editorial hypothesis with no amyloid-negative aging baseline. The biomarker language attached to uncorrected group-level correlations from cohorts of 18, and the presentation of virtual Memantine as pharmacodynamic evidence, overstate what the data support. We therefore conclude that this body of work is best positioned not as established E/I-biomarker science but as a convergent theoretical framework and a structured roadmap of open questions—valuable for organizing hypotheses and guiding the same-subject, in-band-verified, amyloid-baseline-anchored, longitudinally validated studies that the field now requires to convert plausibility into evidence (Lu, 2022; Stefanovski, 2021).

### Open Questions (for follow-up study design)

1. Do AD pathological nodes (anterior theta hypersynchrony, posterior alpha hyposynchrony) ever remain within the mean-field validity band (lambda ≈ 0.70–0.85), and if not, how large is the resulting inference error against spiking ground truth?
2. Can the Stuart-Landau bifurcation parameter a, Jansen-Rit tau_i, and Wong-Wang E-E/E-I/I-E couplings be shown, head-to-head, to capture the same physical E/I shift, and is there a formal mapping among lambda, Yokoyama's mE/I = A/(A+B), and Stam's a?
3. Is the four-parameter Zimmermann (2018) inversion practically identifiable (posterior covariance, profile likelihood) once conduction delays are added, or are the parameter–cognition relationships artifacts of a degenerate manifold?
4. What is the normal-aging E/I and spectral trajectory in amyloid-negative, age-matched controls, and does aMCI in fact show the highest parameter variance as a measured compensation-versus-collapse transition?
5. Does the dual-hit Abeta+Tau forward model (Stefanovski, 2021) outperform Abeta-only modelling against real same-subject EEG/MEG, and how much of the slowing attributed to Abeta is actually Tau-driven disconnection?
6. Does lengthening tau_i represent disinhibition or increased absolute inhibition, and what relative local-excitability index correctly reads hyperexcitability given the increased area under the inhibitory PSC curve?
7. How much of the AD-specific variance in BOLD-based NMM parameter fits (Zimmermann, 2018) reflects neurovascular/cerebrovascular confound rather than neural E/I?
8. Do Cooray (2023) dwell-time/exit-time biomarkers, computed on real AD electrophysiology, distinguish disease stages, and can data assimilation (Yokoyama, 2023) be extended to multi-channel whole-brain waking AD recordings?
9. Does virtual Memantine spectral normalization predict actual drug response in a dose-response, clinically validated dataset, and does theta-AECc stratification improve anti-hyperexcitability trial outcomes (e.g., Levetiracetam)?
10. Can any core finding (theta AECc, embeddedness, tau_i–SUVR mapping) be replicated across independent cohorts, scanners, and atlases with deposited code and inferential statistics?
